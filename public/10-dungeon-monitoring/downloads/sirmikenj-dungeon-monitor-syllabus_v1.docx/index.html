Roles of the DM

  1. Safety patrol ‚Äì as the DM you are responsible for the safety of all the attendees and well as protecting the space.

    Safety means you are protecting both the mental and physical well being of the attendees. You do this by learning the basics of various forms of play. For example needle play might not be your jam but knowing that leaving uncapped needles around is a safety hazard. Today you will learn a little bit about the most common types of play.

    Protecting someone‚Äôs mental well being may involve stopping a scene that they want out of, settling a disagreement between attendees or doing something as simple as chatting with someone to tell them you were impressed with their scene (in the right time and context).

    You are also protecting the space both for the venue owners and the participants. An example is someone who wants to modify a piece of equipment that may lead to unsafe conditions. Another example is notifying folks about a spill that has caused a slippery floor.

  2. You are the information center‚Ä¶people will ask you ‚Äúwhere is

    ?‚Äù, and you should be able to tell them or direct them to someone who can tell them.

  3. Emergency manager- in the event of an emergency evacuation you are responsible for making sure everyone knows where the exits are and where the gathering point is.

    1. If emergency services are coming into the space it is up to the DM to notify everyone that they are coming and to get dressed and put their toys away.

  4. Peacekeeper ‚Äì the DM may be needed to settle disputes of who gets to use equipment or if someone has been using equipment for too long to encourage them to finish up. Most venues use about 90 mins for most scenes although that can vary widely from venue to venue and play to play. Rope tends to take much longer than an impact scene, generally.

  5. Eyes and ears of the venue owner. Here you need to be familiar with the venue’s rules and TOU. For example many venues forbid photos. Some do not allow food in the dungeon.

  6. Relayer of information to the relief DM and or the venue owner. Talk to them about people who are questionable or scenes that may have you uncomfortable.

  7. Etiquette manager ‚Äì dungeon etiquette is relatively standard.

    1. Clean your equipment after use.
    2. Move your toys out of the way for the next person.
    3. Toy bags should not block walk ways or doors.
    4. Volume control (voice and music)
    5. Their scene should not roll into someone else‚Äôs.

  8. Crowd control ‚Äì a really hot scene can draw a crowd of people. The DM may have to encourage them to leave the players some space and refrain from comments and jumping in.

  9. Monitor after care areas

    2. Some places allow sex.
    3. Volume control

    Equipment issues and position

  17. The DM should inspect all the equipment in the play space looking for:

    1. Where is the equipment located‚Ä¶.is there enough space? A good rule of thumb is that there should be enough space to go completely around an equipment station plus double the length of the type of toys that are likely to be used at the station.
    2. Make sure people are aware to check in with the DM and/or the venue’s owner/operator before moving equipment. This should also be conveyed to the relief DM.
      1. If equipment is moved the DM should give it a quick check to make sure it is in good condition and perhaps move it back to where it originated.
    3. Each piece of equipment should be checked for:
      1. Screws and bolts are secure.
      2. Padding is not torn.
      3. Wood is smooth and sealed with no stains/splinters or cracks.
      4. The item is stable and weight bearing.

  18. There is a problem with the equipment‚Ä¶what do I do?

    1. Fix it‚Ä¶.loose bolts and nuts can be easily tightened.
    2. Notify the venue owner.
    3. If the item is unsafe for use, move it out of the way and place a note on it.
    4. Keep an eye on it during the event.

Bystander intervention

On occasion a bystander may intervene in a scene. In the event this occurs:
  1. Call for assistance from another DM or the owners
  2. Typically, the top will be irritated and irate and the bottom will be upset or irate.
  3. Separate the bystander from the scene and get all the details.
  4. If the scene warrants being stopped, call the scene.
  5. If the determination is made that the scene can continue, ask the bystander to move away reassuring them that the DM‚Äôs will monitor the scene.
  6. Refer the parties involved to the owner for determination.

How to observe without being intrusive

  1. Be quiet. Keeping radio volumes on low and phones on silent.
    2. Minimize conversation and avoid conversations with the scene participants unless absolutely critical.
    3. Observe from a distance but close enough that you can still see.
    4. Keep moving‚Ä¶there are other scenes to keep an eye on.
    5. Make sure you have introduced yourself before the scene begins. If you are interested in the scene wait until after care is completed.
    6. If it is a darkened area use the flashlight as little as possible.

  2. Someone playing under the influence:

    1. Playing under the influence was very common in the 70‚Äôs and early 80‚Äôs when dungeons were often located in the basement of bars.
      2. Dangers:
      1. Loss of control‚Ä¶aim can be off completely
      2. Loss of sensation means that someone cannot say when something is too much.
      3. Unable to maintain an adequate airway.
      4. Unable to react in an emergency.
      5. Signs and symptoms:

        1. Odor

        2. Slurred speech

        3. Glassy blood shot eyes

        4. Uncoordinated motor functions

        5. Mood swings

          What to do: Call for assistance
          Stop a scene
          Engage in calm conversation
          Let the group owner decide.
          Some venues have a breathalyzer.
          Marijuana can be ingested (edibles/gummies) smoked or vaped. It can be extremely difficult to identify. Use the guide above.

          A good rule of thumb is that if they look uncoordinated or disoriented they should not be playing.

Dungeon diplomacy:

The role of the DM is not to correct or instruct in a scene but to ensure that no one gets hurt and nothing gets damaged.

Some common disputes that occur:

  1. I was waiting to use that piece of equipment.
  2. They are in the way of my scene.
  3. They are making too much noise
  4. Why can‚Äôt I‚Ä¶.?
  5. I know what I am doing‚Ä¶leave me alone.
  6. Partner disputes.

Separate the parties and get one story at a time.

Low and steady voice. Raising your voice escalates the situation.

Do not make promises or agree with either party. Remaining impartial increases the perception of your authority.

Call for a mod on duty.

Use common sense.

Scenes

What to look for and be aware of:

  1. Impact

    1. Impact is defined as using anything to strike a person. It can be hands, whips, floggers, paddles etc. This can include household and sports items.
      1. Is there enough room for the item being used‚Ä¶if not can room be made?
      2. Is the item being used likely to cause serious injury or death (sledge hammer)?
      3. Is the bottom in obvious distress that is above scene level?
      4. Are parts of the body being impacted that are not safe for the implement? Ie: Whips to the throat‚Ä¶paddles to the head.
      5. Is the bottom obviously injured?

  2. Bondage

    1. Bondage is defined as causing a person to temporarily lose the ability to move or speak independently of the top?

      1. If gags are being used is the bottom able to breathe adequately? Look for cyanosis.

      2. Is the bondage involving the neck? Is it attached to a fixed point?

      3. If the person has a fall, is there adequate padding (mats etc).

      4. Look at the extremities for signs of a blueish tint (note that some bondage can cause a temporary bluing of the skin and still be safe.

      5. In melanated people look for a graying or blanching.

      6. Are any joints being hyper extended to the point of being able to cause serious injury?

      7. Are there adequate safety tools present?

        This usually means shears or quick release snaps.

        If doing chain bondage are bolt cutters available?

  3. Fire scenes

    1. Is this in a designated location?
    2. Is there a fire blanket available?
    3. Is there adequate space between the flame source and the alcohol?
    4. Is the fuel container stable and is the table or stand stable?
    5. Is there a fire extinguisher available? And is it charged?
    6. Is there adequate space for egress in the event of an emergency?
    7. Smoke alarm? Might need to be covered.
    8. If doing cupping is there a broom and dustpan available in the event of glass breakage?
    9. If doing wax play:
      1. Is there something down to catch the wax?
      2. Gel candles ? (NO NO)
      3. Metal fragments in a candle (NO NO)

  4. Waterboarding:

    1. High risk behavior needs close monitoring.
      1. 2 methods are most commonly used:
        1. Dunking in water
        2. Towel wrapped around the head and water is poured over the towel.
    2. Signs and symptoms of hypoxia
      1. Central cyanosis
      2. The body becomes ridged and or clenched.
      3. Weak or ineffective flailing
      4. Feet are drumming.
    3. Is the top strong enough to get an unconscious bottom out of danger.
      1. CPR or mouth to mouth may be needed.
      2. Call 911

  5. Breath play:

    1. The most common technique is manual asphyxia (hand or gag obstructing airway.
    2. Also be associated with gas mask type devices or hoods.
    3. See water boarding for signs and symptoms of hypoxia.
    4. Passing out is common.
    5. Anything around the neck and attached to a fixed object is a no no,
    6. Are shears handy?

  6. Needles and sharp pokey things:

    1. Is this a designated area?
    2. Barrier between furniture and bottom
    3. Container for needles and staples
    4. Product to clean skin

  7. Knives are a highly technical form of play that need a great deal of practice and generally space.

    1. Are the players planning to draw blood?
    2. Barrier needed?
    3. Jabbing at the core is a no no‚Ä¶risk is too high.
    4. Frequently done to a dance such as a tango?
    5. Excessive bleeding is a concern‚Ä¶rule of thumb is if the blood is running down the body or pooling that is too much bleeding.
    6. If the skin is opened enough that you can see a wound it is too much.
    7. Frequently leads to sex.

  8. Rope and suspensions

    1. Floor ties
      1. Is there sufficient padding?
      2. Are there any ropes that end up around the neck?
      3. Is the person to be tethered to a fixed object?
      4. Does the positioning impede breathing?
      5. Are there knots tied over major arteries or known nerve bundles?
      6. Are the extremities turning blue or ashy?
      7. Shears
    2. Suspensions
      1. Is the equipment proper and in good working condition?
      2. Is the rope appropriate for suspension?
      3. Floor padding
      4. Position going to impede breathing?
      5. Quick snap releases?
      6. Shears

  9. Medical play

    1. Medical play involves simulation of healthcare professionals and/or patients.
    2. Play can consist of medical exams, gynecological exams, enemas, sounding or catheterization etc.
    3. Unless someone is in extreme distress there is not much reason to stop the scene.
    4. These scenes frequently lead to sexual activity.

  10. Vac beds or tents
    1. Usually made of a rubber or rubber like latex material.
    2. If the head is covered an unobstructed breathing tube must be present. (See breath play above).
    3. Special attention is required for any vac bed that allows the head to be exposed‚Ä¶these can compress the neck.

Venue roles and responsibilities

  1. Venues are required to maintain a safe properly lit establishment.
    2. Venues can decide who can come in.
    3. Venues can remove anyone at any time for any reason.
    4. Adequate life and safety standards need to be adhered to.

    The DM is the eyes and ears of the venue owner. Proper lighting where appropriate, visible fire exits and a clear emergency plan should all be known to the DM. Take the time to review the plans with your venue owner.

    Basic first aid:

  2. Blood borne pathogens protection. Gloves at the least.

  3. Most common issues a DM will encounter will be:

    1. Minor cuts ‚Äì

      1. Control the bleeding by using a clean dry cloth and putting pressure on it.
      2. Have it cleaned off (soap and water works)
      3. Usually a band aid will suffice.

    2. Major bleeding ‚Äì

      1. Direct pressure (it bleeds through add another one on top of it.
      2. Elevate the extremity
      3. Tourniquet as needed.
      4. If it needs a tourniquet it needs 911.

    3. Spurting blood ‚Äì

      i. Same as above but immediately call 911.

    d. Rope burn-

    i. usually being washed off to clean. OTC creams to soothe the burn (Vaseline, A+D etc)

    e. Twisted ankles ‚Äì

    i. take the weight off, elevate, ice. If still not weight bearing go to urgent care or the ER

    1. Minor burns ‚Äì
      1. cool water not cold
      2. OTC creams as needed for minor pain relief.
      3. If blistered do not pop blisters.
    2. Chemical irritants -
      1. Flush with lots of cool water.
      2. Read the label.
      3. Dawn dish detergent works wonders to help bind with most irritants.
    3. Asthma attacks ‚Äì
      1. Sit down.
      2. Loosen anything restrictive around the chest and abdomen.
      3. Assist with meds.
      4. If cyanosis (blue or gray appearance of lips or pale gums or mucous membranes are present call 911
    4. Fainting ‚Äì
      1. Get them out of whatever position they are in.
      2. Lay them down
      3. Make sure they are breathing
      4. Loosen any tight clothing.
      5. Place in the recovery position (demonstrate)
      6. If not self correcting within 1 minute call 911
    5. Not breathing-
      1. Call for help and call 911.
      2. On their back on a hard surface
      3. Expose the chest
      4. Initial CPR
      5. One hand on top of another on the lower third of the breast bone.
      6. Press down hard and fast.
      7. When tired (about 2 mins) switch.

Someone asks for help‚Ä¶now what?

  1. Evaluate the request
    1. If it is participating in a scene, politely decline.
    2. If someone needs assistance or does not feel safe
      1. Ask them if they want to speak privately but remain in view of non participants.
      2. If they feel unsafe or want to complain about something that has happened to them, call for a MOD.
      3. If they are expressing concern about someone else‚Äôs scene, thank them and let them know you will look at it.
      4. Noise, light, smell (except for gas or burning) complaints are the same as above.
      5. Maintain confidentiality. Notifying the MOD or owner does not break confidentiality.
      6. If some one reports a sexual assault notify the MOD, venue owner, and the police.
